In May 2025, after several trips that had turned up nothing, I finally found Roell's brotherella moss (*Brotherella roellii*) growing on an old-growth western redcedar snag at the Cheakamus Centre in Squamish, BC. The site alone was worth the search—like an ancient ruin, cast in rays of light which beamed down through a gap in the canopy.

*Brotherella roellii* is endangered in Canada, and every population known to still survive is Canadian. It was once also known from Washington State, but those populations are now believed extirpated, leaving a scatter of populations across the Fraser Valley and Howe Sound, in BC's Lower Mainland, as the species' entire remaining range.

This particular population was first recorded in 1916 and last seen in 2006. Confirming it was still there—healthy, and possibly one of the most robust populations known in the region—was great news for the Átl'ka7tsem/Howe Sound Biosphere Region. The species appears to be gone from at least one of its historical sites elsewhere in Squamish, which makes protected refuges like the Cheakamus Centre increasingly important as development continues to fragment lowland coastal rainforests in British Columbia.

Since then, working with Emma Menchions (UBC), we've been investigating how light, moisture, and forest structure interact to shape where the moss can persist.

iNaturalist featured the observation as an [Observation of the Day](https://www.inaturalist.org/observations/282952652), and the find later helped contribute to the Squamish River watershed's recognition as a [Key Biodiversity Area of global significance](https://wcscanada.org/newsroom/news/squamish-river-recognized-as-a-key-biodiversity-area-of-global-importance/)—owed in part to the region holding roughly 10% of this species' entire known population.
