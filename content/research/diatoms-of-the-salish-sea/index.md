We've compiled a first consolidated baseline of diatom diversity for the Salish Sea — [a checklist](https://bdj.pensoft.net/article/189060/) of 924 taxa, published in the open-access *Biodiversity Data Journal* with Mark Webber, Elaine Humphrey, Arjan van Asselt, Alice Chang, and Evan Morian. Diatoms are photosynthetic microalgae that form the base of marine food webs, including the fisheries Coast Salish peoples have relied on for millennia; invisible to the naked eye, they are sensitive to environmental change and useful indicators of ecosystem health.

The region's marine biodiversity is well studied, but research on its primary producers had been scattered, with no consolidated baseline until now. Sampling was concentrated around [Galiano Island](http://biogaliano.org), within Coast Salish territory and a region of some nine million people under growing pressure from urbanization, industry, and shipping. The dataset gives researchers and policymakers a reference point for tracking environmental change there.

Many additions are small, cryptic taxa — including the recently described *Andrzeja fenestrata*, pictured above — found by combining morphological and molecular methods. Community science generates such data, but much of it never reaches the peer-reviewed literature; sustained collaboration between community scientists and research institutes helps bridge that gap, pairing local observation with access to microscopy and molecular tools.
